Court upholds $37 MILLION payout to woman who thought Marlboro Lights were less dangerous than Reds

August 29, 2024
blog

Massachusetts' Supreme Judicial Court upheld a nearly $37million judgment for a woman who claimed she developed lung cancer after switching from Marlboro Red cigarettes to Marlboro Lights because she thought they were less dangerous.

The judicial body delivered a unanimous ruling Tuesday and said Patricia Walsh Greene, 64, might have smoked less or quit sooner if she had not been convinced by Philip Morris' claim that Marlboro Lights were safer.

In its decision, the court wrote: 'Philip Morris represented that such products, including Marlboro Lights, delivered lower tar and nicotine and were a healthier alternative to regular cigarettes.'

However, Philip Morris never told its loyal consumers that internal research conducted by the company in the late 1970s showed that the smoke from Marlboro Light cigarettes was actually worse.

The research found that the smoke was more 'mutagenic' from light cigarettes - meaning it can damage DNA, the first step toward developing cancer.

Her lawyers praised the decision and hoped it helped others, saying: 'I only hope it gives pause to Philip Morris and encourages others like Patti to come forward to call them out for what they did.'

Patricia Walsh Greene was awarded a $37million judgment from a Massachusetts court after suing Philip Morris in 2015 for the lung cancer and subsequent health problems she developed after switching from Marlboro Reds to Lights, thinking they were less harmful

Greene, who was born in the late 1950's, grew up surrounded by sexy and aggressive advertising for cigarettes.

Why are 'light'  cigarettes just as harmful as regulars

Light cigarettes have lower levels of tar, nicotine and other chemicals that can cause harm.

The filters for these cigarettes have small holes in them that allow for more air in each puff.

The National Cancer Institute (NCI) warns that there is no evidence showing a light cigarette is less harmful to use than a regular one.

It warns that many smokers take longer, deeper and more frequent puffs on light cigarettes, offsetting the lower amount of toxins inhaled each time.

A 2017 study by researchers at Harvard University found that there was no difference in death risk depending on a type of cigarette someone smoked.

In 2017, Ohio State University researchers found that light cigarette smokers were actually at a higher risk of developing cancer.

The scientists pointed to the changed filter in these devices altering the way tobacco and other toxins in the products burned.

As a result, they became more dangerous. 

Health experts warn that using any type of nicotine or tobacco product greatly increases a person's risk of developing cancer, heart disease and other deadly conditions.

She smoked her first Marlboro in 1971, when she was 13 years old, according to the court. By high school, she was smoking a pack a day.

According to her attorneys, Greene tried to quit on many occasions using several methods, including nicotine patches and hypnotism.

Between 1979 and 1980, Greene quit smoking for nine months. When that pause ended, she resumed smoking, but switched to Marlboro Lights - from Reds - after seeing advertising assuring that they contained less tar and nicotine.

Greene smoked a pack a day of Marlboro Lights until she quit for good in 1995.

In 2013, Greene was diagnosed with lung cancer, which by 2018 had spread to her brain. She required multiple surgeries and radiation, according to the court.

Eight years ago, in 2015, Greene sued Philip Morris with the help of the Public Health Advocacy Institute. In 2021, the company was found liable for civil conspiracy and Greene was awarded the initial multi-million dollar judgment.

Philip Morris, which is owned by Altria, appealed the ruling, arguing there was insufficient evidence to support the judgments against it, and saying that the interest rates that resulted in the final award amount were excessive.

This week's ruling upheld the original decision and confirmed the jury did have sufficient evidence of Philip Morris conspiring with other cigarette manufacturers in a manner that did ultimately cause Greene's cancer and subsequent kidney failure.

The court found the conspiracy started in 1953 with cigarette executives creating a plan to 'overwhelm' voices of those who challenged cigarettes as unhealthy, the Supreme Court found. 

Companies then spent decades denying  that smoking was addictive and caused cancer, despite internal findings showing different results, the Supreme Court's ruling noted. 

That campaign lasted through Greene's youth.

Companies then developed 'light cigarettes,' which customers believed to be healthier, according to the ruling.

However, according to its own internal research, light cigarettes were more likely to lead to cancer because of the 'mutagenic' properties of light cigarettes, which leads to the DNA-altering process that is a precursor to cancer.

Those findings were not shared with the public.  

Greene smoked her first Marlboro in 1971, when she was just 13, and would be unable to quit until 1995

Greene's original complaint against Philip Morris USA that resulted, in 2021, with a judgment of $37million for the plaintiff. Philip Morris appealed the case and lost again.

Research conducted by Philip Morris in the 70s found that the smoke from Marlboro Light cigarettes was actually more likely to cause the DNA damage that can ultimately lead to cancer

The trial court found that Green's motivation to quit smoking was lowered because she believed Phillip Morris' efforts that light cigarettes were healthier. 

Phillip Morris knew that smokers were smoking more light cigarettes, yet they still believed they were an alternative to quitting. 

The trial court found in favor of Greene and the decision was appealed to the state's highest court. 

One of Greene's PHAI attorneys, Mark Gottlieb, told the Daily Mail that his client is of course happy with the ruling, and that 'it was amazing that she lived to tell her story to the jury at all and unexpected that she survived to see the verdict upheld.'

'It is truly a sad story and I know she would have gladly traded this monetary award for her health. I only hope it gives pause to Philip Morris and encourages others like Patti to come forward to call them out for what they did,' he said. 

About six months after the trial, Greene's husband, who had delivered testimony, died after a short cancer battle.

Since his passing, Greene has been forced to rely on her daughter to bring her to the many medical appointments - something her husband used to do.

Gottlieb said that from his perspective, 'holding a cigarette company responsible for giving free samples of a deadly and addictive product to kids in Boston, while spending tens of millions of dollars each year to lie to the public about what it knew, and selling a product called “Lights” as better for you while knowing it was likely to cause more cancer than its regular cigarettes, shouldn’t take 8 years, hundreds of attorney hours and hundreds of thousands of dollars in expenses.' 

Altria, the parent company to Phillip Morris, did not respond to request for comment.  

Landmark moments in the fight against Big Tobacco

  • 1953: A landmark study by American medical researcher Ernst Wydner shows that painting cigarette tar on the backs of mice produces tumors.
  • 1964: US surgeon general Luther Terry issues the first surgeon general report about the health risks associated with smoking
  • 1965: Congress passes the Federal Cigarette Labeling and Advertising Act, which mandated a surgeon general's warning on packs of cigarettes.
  • 1971: All broadcast advertising for cigarettes is banned.
  • 1982: US Surgeon General C. Everett Koop finds that secondhand smoke may cause lung cancer.
  • 1990: Mississippi attorney Don Barrett wins a case against the tobacco industry. Smoking is banned on passenger flights of longer than six hours.
  • 1992: U.S. Supreme Court rules that 1965 act does not shield tobacco companies from lawsuits.
  • July 1992: Wayne McLaren, the model for the 'Marlboro Man' dies of lung cancer. 
  • February 1994: FDA Commission David Kessler announced plans to regulate tobacco like a drug. 
  • March 1994: A national class-action lawsuit is filed on behalf of a Louisiana attorney who died of lung cancer. 
  • April 1994: Seven executives from leading tobacco companies testify before Congressman Henry Waxman's committee that they believe 'Nicotine is not addictive.'
  • July 1994: The U.S. Justice Department opens a criminal investigation into possible perjury by tobacco executives during their testimony before Congress. 
  • January 1998: Big Tobacco executives testify before Congress that nicotine is addictive and smoking may cause cancer. 
  • 1998: Tobacco Master Settlement Agreement. Attorneys general from 46 states sued the four major US tobacco companies for the recovery of their Medicaid costs pertaining to tobacco-related health care costs. 
  • 2006: American Cancer Society vs. Big Tobacco. The American Cancer Society and other plaintiffs won a major case against Big Tobacco after a judge found that tobacco companies were guilty of lying to the American public about the deadly effects of cigarettes and secondhand smoke.
