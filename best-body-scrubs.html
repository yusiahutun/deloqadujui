Your skincare routine is likely on auto pilot at this point: Cleanse, exfoliate, hydrate, repeat. Consistency is key for glowing skin. The same goes for your skin below the chin. Type of Exfoliant

Exfoliants come in two types: Chemical and physical. “Chemical scrubs are typically formulated with an acid (such as alpha or beta-hydroxy acids) or an enzyme to break down skin cells, whereas physical scrubs use small particles or a textured surface to scrub away dead skin and debris,” explains board-certified dermatologist Dr. Lauren Hoffman. Chemical options, such as First Aid Beauty KP Bump Eraser Body Scrub with 10% AHA and Kopari KP Body Bumps Be Gone with 10% AHA Clarifying Body Scrub, “can be used to treat acne, acne scars, hyperpigmentation, melasma, and fine wrinkles” she adds, as “AHAs are water soluble and can’t penetrate the skin very deeply” and even “have anti-inflammatory and antibacterial properties.” Due to its properties, this ingredient can be found in many sensitive skin exfoliators.

Physical exfoliants, like Fresh Brown Sugar Body Polish Exfoliator and OUAI Cleansing Scalp & Body Sugar Scrub, use small particles to remove the outermost layer of your skin. This type of exfoliant is extremely effective at revealing radiant skin, but because it can be abrasive, should be used only once a week. “If used too aggressively, it can lead to micro-tears in the skin and lead to redness and irritation. Darker skin tones have to be especially cautious of this,” says Dr. Hoffman.

Scent

You will notice that most body scrubs, with the exception of First Aid Beauty KP Bump Eraser Body Scrub with 10% AHA and Necessaire Body Exfoliator (which has a fragrance-free option) are scented. Though many dermatologists say that those with sensitive skin should avoid fragranced products, a lot of the fragrance in the scrubs above come from the natural ingredients already included, like fruits and coffee, rather than an added scent. For example, we tested Avene Gentle Scrub on eczema-prone skin, and found that it did not irritate it in the slightest. If you are worried, however, then Dr. Hoffman recommends patch testing the product on the back of your wrist to see if you experience any adverse reactions.

Your Questions, Answered

What is a body scrub?

“A body scrub is a mechanical exfoliant that uses friction to exfoliate the skin,” explains board-certified dermatologist Dr. Carmen Castilla. “They help to remove dead skin cells on the skin’s surface.” The end result reveals smooth, radiant skin that looks overall refreshed.

How do you use body scrub?

To begin, make sure you are soaking in warm water – this helps to slightly open your pores and allow the exfoliant to reach hidden dirt. Scoop out a tablespoon of product on your hand, and with either your hand, a loofa, or exfoliating mitten, rub it around your chosen skin surface in circular motions. After about 30 seconds, rinse off the scrub thoroughly and follow up with a lotion to lock in moisture.

Should I use body scrub before or after shaving?

Using a body scrub before shaving is a great way to adequately prep your skin. “Exfoliating before shaving can help remove dead skin cells and lead to a closer shave,” says Dr. Castilla. While most of the time it’s safe to use a scrub after shaving as well, she also warns that “it can cause irritation.” The safest time to use a body scrub after shaving is “a day or two later as it can help prevent ingrown hairs.”

How often can I use body scrub?

The answer to this will vary depending on your skin type and personal needs, but traditional body scrubs – meaning those with physical exfoliating particles, like Fresh Brown Sugar Body Polish Exfoliator and OSEA Salts of the Earth Body Scrub – should only be used once or twice a week, says Dr. Castilla. The products that can be used more frequently will provide clear instructions and are typically the gentler options, such as Kiehl's Soy Milk & Honey Body Polish, or SNP Cafferonic Body Scrub, which we used every other day in our test.

“Using too much of a scrub can break down the skin’s protective barrier and put you at risk for sun damage, dryness, or infection,” says Dr. Hoffman, so make sure to follow the instructions included on the packaging.

Can I use body scrub on my face?

Body scrubs should never be used on the face. “This is a definite no no,” says Dr. Castilla. “Scrubs formulated for the body are too harsh to be used on the face.” Dr. Chan agrees, saying, “facial skin is much thinner and more sensitive than skin on the trunk, arms, and legs.”
