Pennsylvania man who murdered his mom had attacked his girlfriend and a Lyft driver weeks earlier

July 07, 2024
blog

David Sumney, 33, now faces a maximum of 20 to 40 years behind bars for third-degree murder after prosecutors downgraded his charges earlier this month 

A Pennsylvania man who murdered his mother in her bathtub before taking selfies covered in her blood, had allegedly strangled and waterboarded his girlfriend in a hotel just six weeks prior, DailyMail.com can reveal. 

David Sumney, 33, despite his violent past including attacks on both parents, managed to evade capture in the summer of 2019, after New Jersey police failed to enter his arrest warrant into a national crime database. 

About two months before his mother's gruesome death, Sumney's ex-girlfriend  claimed she was tortured by the accused killer for three days in a hotel in Atlantic City, New Jersey, before being saved by a bellhop who found her in the room after the pair failed to check out on July 16, 2019. 

Local police were quick to identify the suspect and obtained a warrant to arrest Sumney on attempted murder the same day, but never acted upon it. 

The victim, who did not wish to be named, and Sumney's family said he instead managed to slip through the cracks after authorities failed to log the warrant into the National Crime Information Center, which would have alerted out-of-state police to arrest him.

Sumney was then able to leave New Jersey and flew to Denver before returning to his home state of Pennsylvania, they told DailyMail.com.    

Sumney pleaded guilty to murdering his mother, Margaret Sumney, 67, who was found dead in her bathtub at her South Fayette, Pennsylvania home on September 2, 2019

He is alleged to have snapped a commemorative selfie of his own blood-smeared face, flashing a 'thumbs up' sign, and video of him washing his mother's mangled corpse in the bathtub where she was found 

There, he would go on to face another run-in with police after attacking a Lyft driver a month later – but his outstanding warrant went unnoticed.  

Just a few days after that, he killed his own 67-year-old mother, Margaret Sumney, at her Pennsylvania home. 

He snapped a commemorative selfie of his own blood-smeared face, flashing a 'thumbs up' sign, and video of him washing his mother's mangled corpse in the bathtub where she was found.

But according to the family, the greatest injustice came in court this month, when the prosecutor pursuing the murder case agreed to drop the top murder charge, telling the judge he had the family's backing.

'That was a lie,' Margaret's sister Ann Shade, 72, told DailyMail.com. 'Most of the family is against this. I wanted to stand up and yell 'no!' but I wasn't allowed to say anything. I was shaking my head, trying to keep myself in my seat.'

Family sources say Sumney had a history of being violent towards his parents (pictured) but avoided jail time, in part, because his mother would urge leniency

Now the family's facing a new fear – that Sumney will come after them when he gets out, thanks to the DA's decision to drop the first-degree murder charge and a litany of lesser offenses. 

Sumney appeared in court on August 17, pleading guilty to third-degree murder. 

He had been originally charged with homicide as well as abuse of a corpse, robbery and theft by unlawful taking, but those charges were later dropped. 

If he had been convicted of the more severe charges, he would receive a mandatory life sentence. 

Instead, he now faces a maximum of 20 to 40 years behind bars when he is sentenced in Judge Edward Borkowski’s courtroom November 17 on a single charge of third-degree murder.

That means Sumney, who has spent three years in jail awaiting trial, could get released in 17 years, at age 51.

'When all this happened to me, I felt almost like prey, like I was being hunted,' Sumney's ex-girlfriend told DailyMail.com. 

'And I cannot go back to feeling like that, I just can't. It's not possible for my mental health.'

'We're all fearful that he'll get out because he could not be rehabbed,' she continued. 

'He's a multiple violent offender. And now he might get out at 51 and do the same thing again. I'm just very disenfranchised with the criminal justice system.'

On August 24, weeks after the alleged attack on his ex, police just outside Pittsburgh arrested Sumney for allegedly assaulting a Lyft driver who got lost after he punched him in the face and scratched his arm. 

Officers issued a summons charging him with simple assault and harassment and physical strike, were not aware of the warrant issued by the neighboring state.

It was only when his mother was murdered that local authorities noticed the open case.

Police found Margaret Sumney's beaten and lacerated body in a bathtub on September 2, 2019, two days after her son took the grisly photos.

Her family had tried to contact her for days to let her know that her brother had died, but she didn't answer, according to the criminal complaint. 

One of her two daughters called police that morning asking them to do a wellness check, while informing them that David Sumney was staying in the house and had a history of elder abuse.

In July 2015, he was charged with assaulting his father. In August 2014 and November 2016, he was charged with assaulting his mom. But according to his family, charges were downgraded in those cases as well to misdemeanor assault and harassment.

Margaret Sumney described the November 2016 attack on her in a hand-written note to police, obtained by Daily Mail.com.

She wrote that 'he tried to kill me,' 'punched my face and body, choked me, pulled my hand across my body,' and told her 'I'll get you next time.'

She noted in the report that he was on probation for a similar attack. And bizarrely, she mentioned that her son was a Navy Seal. A family member told DailyMail.com that detectives later determined he was lying about his military service.

But Sumney largely avoided jail time, in part, because his mother would urge leniency.

'My sister (Margaret) would plead to the judge and say, 'well, if he gets rehabilitation,' and the judge would go easy on him,' Shade told DailyMail.com. 

'She was trying to get any kind of help she could get him. She loved him, tried to do her best, and gave him every single thing she could till there was nothing left. It was like she was the battered wife. 

'She was in the hospital a couple times and would not want to say that David put her there. If she didn't do what he wanted, he'd pull her hair a lot and drag her up the steps.'

Ultimately, Shade, like others in the family who spoke anonymously with DailyMail.com, blamed the criminal justice system.

'He was able to play the system,' Shade said. 'After how many times he'd end up in court, they should have known to keep him locked up for a while.'

'That's why he keeps slipping through the cracks – because of plea deals,' Ellen, one of Margaret's two daughters told DailyMail.com.

On the morning of September 2, Peggy – Margaret's other daughter – called police to do the wellness check. 

Shade called them later that afternoon, but the door was locked so police didn't enter, the complaint states. It wasn't until late that night that Shade had them break in.

Blood was splattered throughout the house in South Fayette, 15 miles southwest of Pittsburgh, including some on the ceiling. 

They later search Sumney's iPhone and found hundreds of photos of the crime scene.

During an August 17 court hearing, the prosecutor said the pathologist likened her injuries to that of a fatal car crash, with lacerations covering her body including her face and scalp, and fractures to her ribs and spine, according to the Pittsburgh Post-Gazette.

His phone's browser revealed he had researched handling a corpse, including 'How long do you wait to dispose of a body?' the paper reported.

Shade, sitting with several other relatives, audibly gasped in the gallery, muttering 'no' when Assistant District Attorney Michael Pradines insisted to the judge that the family largely supports the deal when, in fact, they don't.

Only the victim's youngest daughter, Peggy, signed off, after the prosecution argued this would guarantee he'll have no chance at an appeal. 

'I was shaking my head, trying to keep myself in my seat,' Shade told DailyMail.com.

'Don't say we agreed to the plea when we had no say so,' she added. 'He didn't give us a say so.'

The Allegheny County District Attorney's Office, contacted by DailyMail.com, wouldn't discuss specifics but stood by its decision.
